# OQ-40: UConn Grant PI Strategy

**question_id:** OQ-40
**domain:** grant_research

---

## Recommendation

UConn is well-positioned to serve as PI institution on federal grants. The standard structure: UConn faculty member as Principal Investigator, Cena Health as a subaward recipient providing the intervention and operational data, and UConn's IRB governing the research protocol (already established per OQ-31).

The critical path items are: (1) identify a UConn faculty member willing to serve as PI — this person must have an active faculty appointment (not adjunct/emeritus), a track record of funded research (strengthens the application), and expertise in a relevant domain (nutrition science, public health, health services research, or food systems), (2) confirm that UConn's Office of Sponsored Programs will administer the grant (standard for any UConn faculty-led grant), and (3) execute a subaward agreement between UConn and Cena that defines: what Cena provides (intervention delivery, de-identified operational data, clinical outcome data per IRB protocol), what Cena receives (subaward funding for direct costs of research participation), and intellectual property terms (who owns findings, data, and derivative works).

For NIH grants specifically, the PI must demonstrate institutional support (lab/office space, protected research time, institutional resources). UConn provides all of this. Cena's role as subaward recipient is well-established in clinical research — the PI studies the intervention, the intervention provider (Cena) delivers it under protocol. The data use agreement (OQ-23, confirmed as separate from BAA) governs research data flow.

Target grant mechanisms: NIH R01 (investigator-initiated research), USDA AFRI (food-as-medicine falls within USDA's nutrition research priorities), and PCORI (patient-centered outcomes research — strong fit for food-as-medicine outcomes). Each has different review criteria but the UConn-PI / Cena-subaward structure works for all three.

## Basis

- **NIH Grants Policy Statement (GPS), Section 2.3.1:** The PI must be employed by the applicant organization (UConn) and have the authority, qualifications, and time commitment to direct the research. Subaward recipients (Cena) are not required to have a PI appointment.
- **45 CFR 46 (Common Rule):** The PI's institution (UConn) holds the Federal-Wide Assurance (FWA) and IRB of record. UConn's FWA covers all human subjects research conducted under its auspices — this is already in place (OQ-31 confirmed UConn IRB makes re-consent determinations).
- **UConn institutional infrastructure:** UConn has an active FWA, multiple IRBs, an Office of Sponsored Programs (for grant administration), and departments in nutrition science, public health, and kinesiology — all relevant homes for a food-as-medicine PI.
- **Domain 10.4 (Research Data Management):** The de-identification pipeline and BigQuery research tier are designed to support exactly this structure — UConn accesses de-identified research data via BigQuery, never the clinical database.
- **OQ-23 decision:** Research data use agreement is separate from the BAA. This agreement must be in place before grant-funded data collection begins.

## Alternatives considered

| Alternative | Description | Why not | When it would be preferred |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cena as PI institution | Cena applies for grants directly with a Cena employee as PI | Cena would need its own FWA, IRB (or IRB of record arrangement), and a PI with an academic research track record. This takes 6-12 months to establish and significantly weakens the grant application (no publication record, no institutional research infrastructure, no preliminary data from an academic setting). | If Cena hires a PhD-level research director with an established publication record AND obtains its own FWA — feasible in 12-18 months but not for the first round of grants. |
| Multiple-PI (MPI) structure | UConn faculty as Contact PI, Cena employee as co-PI | NIH allows MPI applications, but the co-PI must meet the same qualifications as the PI. Adds complexity to the application and budget. The subaward structure achieves the same operational arrangement with less administrative overhead. | If Cena has a research-qualified employee (PhD, publication record) who would strengthen the application — the MPI structure gives Cena more visibility and control over the research direction. |
| Cedars-Sinai or Vanderbilt as PI institution | Use a different academic partner for the PI role | These partnerships are earlier stage than UConn. The UConn pilot is the first clinical validation — it generates the preliminary data that makes subsequent grant applications (with any partner) competitive. | For the second round of grants — once UConn pilot data exists, Cedars or Vanderbilt faculty may want to PI their own studies of Cena's intervention in their populations. This is a feature, not a conflict. |
| Skip federal grants, pursue foundation/corporate funding | Avoid the complexity of federal grant applications entirely | Federal grants (NIH R01, USDA AFRI) carry prestige, multi-year funding, and signal clinical legitimacy to payers and partners. Foundation grants are smaller, shorter, and don't carry the same signal. | If the UConn pilot timeline doesn't align with federal grant cycles (NIH R01 has ~9 month review timeline) — foundation grants can bridge the gap. |

## Uncertainty flags

- **Faculty champion identification:** The entire strategy depends on finding a UConn faculty member who wants to study food-as-medicine and is willing to commit PI-level effort (typically 10-25% of their time). Vanessa's relationship with UConn should identify candidates. If no faculty member is interested, this path is blocked.
- **UConn departmental support:** The PI's department must support the grant application (provide resources, approve time commitment). Some departments are more grant-friendly than others.
- **Grant cycle timing:** NIH R01 has three annual deadlines (Feb, Jun, Oct) with ~9 month review. If the UConn pilot starts Q2 2026, the earliest a grant-funded study could begin is ~Q1 2027 (assuming an Oct 2026 submission). This timeline affects what preliminary data is available for the application.
- **Intellectual property:** Grant-funded research generates IP. The subaward agreement must define who owns what — typically, the PI institution owns the research findings, but Cena should retain ownership of its platform, intervention design, and operational data. Negotiate this before the grant application, not after the award.
- **Indirect cost rates:** UConn's F&A (facilities and administrative) rate is set by HHS negotiation — typically 50-60% for on-campus research. This means $0.50-0.60 of every grant dollar goes to UConn overhead. Cena's subaward has its own (lower or zero) indirect rate. Budget accordingly.
